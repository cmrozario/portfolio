Phase Two: Final Design

Design Process & Design Decisions

To answer my question, I wanted to visualize two things: the magnitude of the working class versus the senior class, how these magnitudes have changed in the past 100 years. Throughout phase 1, I found it was simple to put this information into a visualization, but not simple to put JUST this information in the visualization. The first main issue I had to solve was: I did not want to convolve my visualization with population growth data. I do not care about how the population has grown, I only care about its makeup. To do this, I decided to show the breakdown in percentages of the total population for each year instead of using absolute numbers. However, there were some tradeoffs here.

My first instinct was a pie chart. This looked nice and conveyed what I wanted it to, but in class, Prof. Arvind said never to use pie charts as they are interpreted differently by each person. So I came up with the bar chart. Then, I remembered the discussion on how humans can't tell relative magnitudes of bars if they are not aligned on the same axis. So I thought about aligning the three bars on the vertical axis instead. However, this looses the encoded information that these are percent breakdowns of the total population. The reader wouldn't be able to tell that the three stacked bars, when layed out end to end, measured the same length and were not encoding magnitude of population.

I chose to add the percent increase labels to solidify the answer to the main question. I didn't want the reader to have to work to look at the graph then estimate their own answer. I chose the color scheme I did becuase I wanted it to be light on your eyes but still have alot of contrast. Hence the muted primary colors. I chose to group the ages into three buckets; child, working, and senior. To answer my question, you need to aggregate over these fields and I wanted to save the reader the mental math of summing over many bars of the same color. This coarse bucketing obscures specific age population data but that is by design.
