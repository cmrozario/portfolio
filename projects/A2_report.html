Consecrata — crozario

Subtheme: Exploring speculative investment

Overall Analysis Questions

  1. What percent of homes in Boston should be available for rent? In other words, how many people need to rent homes for various reasons rather than buy? I ask this question because Boston is a city with many college students and fresh graduates who are not looking to buy a home. Are speculators who buy and rent homes providing to this demand? Are they over providing?
  2. What are the different investment methods and how do they differ based on the size of the investor? I’ve seen many videos on TikTok/Instagram of people talking about their Airbnb/home rental business and the method they describe is typically putting the smallest percentage down payment possible and using the rent money to pay the mortgage. Are these people and institutional investors competing over the same pool of homes or are the institutional investors typically going after different property types?
  3. How fast are homes typically sold after they are bought? Furthermore, how long do institutional investors hold their investments? After the reading, it’s clear that a there is a huge margin to be made by the institutional investors in flipping homes. But how many homes are bought solely as a “safe investment” and are sitting empty for years?

Discoveries & Insights

Summary

Non-investors contribute the vast majority of transactions under 5 million dollars. Large and institutional investors spend alot of money on Boston real estate but concentrate in high end buildings and commercial spaces. Smaller and micro investors are the entities who are purchasing the properties most likely to be in demand by first time home owners and non-investors. The spatial layout of Boston has little to do with home values. All neighborhoods have a wide distribution of prices of homes leading every type of investor and non-investors to compete for the same properties. Theres evidence of speculatory buying in the small and micro-investors who are purchasing at least their third home. These purchases are not high-end properties and are affordable homes non-investors otherwise would buy. Further analysis using information about homes put up for rent could clearly distinguish these buyers and speculatory.
